Explaining Schizophrenia


Scenario: A 23 year old male has been brought into the hospital by his family members who are worried about him. They say that he is hearing voices telling him that people are trying to kill him. He also feels that people are talking about him. He has stopped going to work few months before onset of illness. He does not use alcohol or any other substance. He has illness since 6 months. Explain the diagnosis to the patient's relative. 


Checklist:


Uses redirection skills

  • Thanks patient for sharing their story with him / her.
  • Explains that the information has provided fair idea about what is going on.


Gets permission to discuss the diagnosis - est, rapport

  • "Would it be okay if we discuss what is my assessment of what might be going on?


Explains schizophrenia

  • Clarifies the relatives understanding 
    • What do you think is wrong with your relative?
  • Explains nature of schizophrenia
    • 'It seems that your relative is having schizophrenia.'
    • 'It is serious mental illness, where the person has difficulty in differentiating real from unreal'
    • 'They may hear voices when no one is around or have visions or other strange sensation '
    • 'They may harbour suspiciousness or other strongly held beliefs that are irrational to other people e.g. getting messages from TV, people can read their thoughts etc.'
    • 'Their thoughts can become jumbled up so that their speech may not make little sense to other people'
    • 'Their behaviour may change and they may stop going to school or work, lack initiative and may not show any emotions.'
    • 'About 1 in 100 are likely to suffer from this illness during their lifetime'
    • 'Males and females are equally affected'
    • 'In males the illness begins between 15-25 and in females between 25-35'
  • If asked about the cause of schizophrenia
    • 'There is no single cause of schizophrenia. 'Like heart disease or diabetes, which are caused by interaction between multiple factors like lifestyle, genetics etc., Schizophrenia is also thought to be caused by multiple factors.' 
    • 'Those destined to develop schizophrenia appear to inherit genes, which cause subtle changes in brain during it’s development.'
    • 'People with these changes when faced with life stresses (immigration etc.), substance abuse, emotional and social problems develop the illness.'
    • 'Current understanding is that schizophrenia is a brain based disorder in which changes in the levels of dopamine and serotonin occur in the areas of the brain'
    • 'In certain cases schizophrenia like illness may be brought on by systemic problems such as stroke, epilepsy, thyroid disorder or medications.'
  • if asked about how it is diagnosed 
    • 'There is no test to diagnose schizophrenia. It is diagnosed based on the history and evaluation of the mental state. However, doctors may advised test to rule out systemic problems.'
  • If asked what can be done to help him
    • 'Hospital admission may be required to ensure safety of the patient during a crisis.' 
    • 'Pharmacological treatment is done using medication called anti-psychotics which bring about changes in levels of dopamine and serotonin.' 
    • 'Talking to a psychologist can help the patient gradually differentiate the real from unreal.'
    • 'Family education teaches skills to family member for better management of the patient, self care and identify early signs of relapse.'
    • 'In self-help groups patients meet others suffering from similar illness and can then share and learn from each other’s experiences.'
    • 'Patients often become ill at a young age and miss out on skills required to lead a productive life. Rehabilitation using vocational training, problem solving and money management skills can make patients equipped to live independently.'
  • If asked whether he will get better 
    • 'It is estimated that around one third of the people diagnosed with schizophrenia experience only one episode, one third have occasional episodes, which are short lived and one third live with schizophrenia as an ongoing problem.'
  • If asked if people with schizophrenia are violent 
    • 'There is no evidence to show that people suffering from schizophrenia are more prone to violence.'
    • 'Often they are troubled and distressed from the derogatory content of their symptoms, which puts them at a higher risk of suicide.'


Assesses patients understanding?

  • 'Does this make sense to you?'
