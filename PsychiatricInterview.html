Psychiatric Interview


What are the elements of a psychiatric assessment? 


The basic psychiatric assessment consists of: 

    1. Taking a psychiatric history (the information you have gathered from the patient, usually under a series of headings) 
    2. Conducting a mental state exam (being able to describe a “snapshot” of how the patient presented across a number of different domains during the interview)
    3. Obtaining collateral information (to obtain different perspectives about the patient’s presentation)


How is psychiatric interview different from medical interview?


The psychiatric history, for the most part, has a structure identical to that of the medical history. The major differences are: 

    1. A greater emphasis on taking a drug and alcohol history 
    2. Taking a forensic history 
    3. Asking about any past psychiatric history (in addition to past medical history) 
    4. Taking a personal history
    5. The history and the process of examination go side-by-side for the major part 


What are the purposes of Psychiatric interview?


    1. To obtain information which can assist with diagnosis, as well as management 
    2. To establish a rapport with the patient 
    3. To understand the patient in the context of their day to day life as well as their past


Some useful communication skills 


A. Introductions


    1. Always introduce yourself to the patient and explain why you are seeing them
    2. It is a good idea to take basic notes while you gather your history, as there is likely to be a lot of information
    3. Explain to the patient why you are taking notes (particularly important for paranoid patients)
    4. Explain to the patient about confidentiality and its limits
    5. Explain to the patient that there is a lot of information to cover and that, at times, you may have to interrupt the patient (if you don’t explain this at the start, you may appear quite rude throughout the interview)


Sample:


    1. “Hi. My name is Dr Kumar. I’m one of the mental health doctors working in the hospital. The emergency doctors tell me that you’ve had a rough time today, and I would like to ask you some questions so I can find out what’s been happening, and how I can help. Would that be ok?” 
    2. “Because I want to learn as much about you as I can, I might interrupt you sometimes, but don’t mean to be rude. I’ll also be writing some notes so I don’t miss anything important” 
    3. “I also tell everyone I see about doctor‐patient confidentiality. What this means is that everything you say to me is private, and I can’t tell  others without your permission. The only exceptions are if I feel your safety or the safety of others is at risk” 
    4. “I’ll start with some basic questions….” 


This sample introduction will need to be adapted to the particular situation e.g. an aggressive patient is unlikely to want to hear this much detail, but easing an anxious patient into an interview may help make them feel more comfortable. 


B. Non-verbal communication

       

    1. Get down to the patient’s level 
    2. Towering above your patient can present as being intimidating and make it difficult to establish a rapport.
    3. That said, if you believe a patient is at risk of becoming violent, it may be worth standing up and staying close to the exit  
    4. It is useful to intermittently show your patient that you’re listening. You may do this by nodding occasionally or using verbal cues in response to statements made by the patient (e.g. saying “ok” to confirm you heard what the patient said or “hmmm” to indicate that you are thinking about what was said)


C. Open-ended questions 


    1. Open questions are questions that invite the patient to talk about their experience in a more detailed manner, and for them to describe what is important to them 
    2. Open questions are useful at the beginning of interviews as the patient can orientate you to their presentation using their own words 
    3. Open questions can help to build rapport as the patient feels “heard” 
    4. Examples: 
      1. “What’s happened for you to come to hospital today?” 
      2. “Can you tell me about anything that’s been making you feel stressed lately?” 
      3. “Can you tell me about how you’ve been feeling recently?”


D. Closed questions 


    1. Closed questions invite more direct answers (often “yes or no” responses), and can be useful for clarifying important information 
    2. They can be useful when establishing a diagnosis, and in establishing whether there are any serious harms identified during an assessment 
    3. Examples: 
      1. “Have you been feeling low in your mood?” 
      2. “Have you been sleeping well?”
      3. “Have you had any thoughts of wanting to hurt yourself?” 


E. Reflections 


    1. Reflections are used by the assessor to “mirror” what emotions the patient has described, to show that you are listening, and to clarify that you are understanding correctly what the patient is saying 
    2. Examples: 
      1. “It seems to me you were feeling like your partner wasn’t listening to you when the two of you were arguing?” 
      2. “It sounds like you were feeling upset with yourself after you cut yourself?” 
    3. There are question marks at the end of these statements, as you are inquiring more about how they felt, rather than telling the patient how they felt 


F. Summarising and checking   


    1. This technique involves summarising the information you have heard, and checking that you have it right 
    2. This can help to make your history is accurate, and helps a patient feel heard 
    3. Particularly useful at the end of interviews 
    4. Examples: 
      1. “From what you’ve said, it sounds like your mood has been low for a few weeks, that you’re having difficulty sleeping, and that you’ve lost a substantial amount of weight. Is that right?” 
      2. “I’m hearing that you’ve felt very low in yourself, and that sometimes you wish you weren’t alive, but have not had any thoughts or plans of hurting yourself. Is that right?”


Staying safe 


    1. The vast majority of mental health patients are no more likely to harm you than their “medical” counterparts 
    2. That said, some patients (e.g. psychotic patients or antisocial patients with a forensic history) may be more likely to, so it is important to think about how you will stay safe before every patient encounter 
    3. Useful tips for staying safe: 
      1. Ideally see patients with someone else in the room (e.g. a member of nursing staff) 
      2. This is particularly important if a patient has sexualised thought content or is disinhibited 
      3. Have security present when interviewing a patient who is very aggressive (e.g. in the emergency department) 
      4. Stay close to the door during the assessment, and make sure you can exit the room unimpeded   
      5. Trust your “gut feeling” i.e. if you are feeling unsafe, then exit the interview in a calm manner 
      6. Call a “code grey” if a patient is very aggressive. 


The Psychiatric History


‘History taking is never a completed task, no matter how long you have known the patient. No one can ever know everything about another human being.


A. Demographic data 


    • Name 
    • Age 
    • Gender 
    • Marital status 
    • Employment status 
    • Occupation 
    • Date of admission (if applicable) 
    • Legal status (if applicable) 


B. Reason for referral


    • How did you come into the hospital? 
    • Who referred you? When? Why? 
    • Did you come of your own free will or were you forced to come?


C. Chief Complaint 


Establish exactly why the patient came to see the psychiatrist, preferably using the patient’s own words.


    • Ask mainly open questions, e.g. Can you tell me why you've come to the hospital today? 
    • Try to establish 
      • Why the individual presented this time?  
      • What events led up to the presentation?


D. History of Presenting Complaints 


    • When did you last feel well? And then what happened? 
    • Identify specific symptoms that are present and their duration
    • Which symptoms started first?
    • How did it start? gradually, rapidly or suddenly?
    • Did it get better, worse, remain same, or was it up and down?
    • What has been the impact of the problem on your normal life and activities?
    • What was going on in your life when the problems started?
    • How is your sleep? How is your appetite? Has their been any change in your weight? 


    • Diagnostic questioning - Try to form a diagnostic hypothesis based on the information collected so far (logico-deductive approach). Based on your hypothesis use appropriate screening questions. [e.g. You think that this patient might be having depression, ask questions about depressive disorder as listed in the section on "Eliciting symptoms of depression"]


    • Psychiatric review of systems - Do a psychiatric review of systems to evaluate for differentials and co-morbidities. Not all of the following questions need to be asked in a particular case. Choose based on the possible differentials and expected co morbidities. However a mnemonic to remember all the categories is DAMP DESPOT DADS. If any of the selected screens is positive, explore further using diagnostic questions for that screened disorder. 


DAMP 

Depression 

    • Have you felt sad or depressed?
    • Have you lost interest in previously pleasurable activities?

                               

Anxiety 

    • GAD - Some people tend to worry excessively, Is that the case for you? Often feel your worry is out of control?
    • Panic attack - Do you have sudden anxiety attacks? 
    • Agoraphobia - Do you avoid going certain places because you are fearful of having a panic attack?
    • Social anxiety - Are you uncomfortable in social situations?
    • Phobias – Do you have any specific fears such as fear of insects or flying?


Mania / Hypomania

    • In the past week, have you felt very cheerful or unusually irritable or over-talkative
    • Have you felt unusually high level of energy and decreased need for sleep


Psychosis - with warning shot (The following questions may sound strange but they are part of my assessment so I am asking them to you.)

    • Have you had a waking dream like experience?
    • Have you had strange experiences?
    • Do you hear of see things that other people can't hear or see?
    • Do you ever feel that people are bothering you or trying to harm you?
    • Do you feel that strangers look at you or make comments about you?

DESPOT

Dissociation

    • Do you ever lose time, forget important details about yourself, or find evidence that you took part in events that you cannot recall?  
    • Do you ever feel that people or places that are familiar to you are unreal?
    • Do you ever feel detached from your body? 


Eating, feeding and Elimination

    • What do you think about your appearance? Do you ever restrict or avoid certain foods so much that it negatively affects your health or weight?
    • Do you have difficulty with control over your bowel or bladder?


Somatic Symptoms – Do you worry a lot about your health?


Personality disorders 

    • Since a young age we develop characteristic ways of thinking, feeling and behaving. 
    • Can you identify particular patterns that have caused significant problems in social and work life? 
    • Such as how you think about self and others, how you feel in response to difficult situations, how you relate to people and ability to control impulses?  


Obsessions - Do you have any unusual or repetitive thoughts that you know are silly but you cannot stop thinking about? Do you feel there are certain rituals you have to do?


Trauma - Do you painful memories or dreams of a terrible experience, such as an accident?

DADS

Dementia - Do you have difficulty with your memory or orientation?


Addictions

    • To better understand your condition I need to know whether you have any addictions. Can I ask you about that?
    • Do you use alcohol, tobacco or any other recreational drug?


Disruptive  

    • Do you have times when you have become so upset that you have made verbal or physical threats? 
    • Have you ever been aggressive to people or animals, destroyed property or stolen things?


Suicidal ideation and other risks  

    • You have been going through a lot.
    • Have you ever felt like life has become a burden? 
    • Do you ever feel you go to sleep and never wake up?
    • Have you ever has thoughts of harming yourself?
    • Did you actually try?
    • How do you feel about it now?
    • Have you made any plans?
    • How close have you come to implementing it? 
    • What did you do? Did you ever try?


E. Past psychiatric history 


    • Have you ever had problems with your mental health? 
    • Have you ever seen a psychiatrist before? 
      • If so, ask about previous psychiatric episodes – the symptoms, precipitants, where they were seen, by whom and the diagnosis, if known. 
      • What treatments have you had before and what was the response?
      • Type of treatment – inpatient/outpatient, informal/detention. 
    • Has there ever been a time when you felt completely well? 
    • Also ask about inter-episode functioning (psychiatric state between episodes – whether completely well or maintained on treatment). 
    • Have you ever attempted to harm yourself in the past? History of overdoses, deliberate self harm (DSH) or attempted suicide.


F. Past medical history 


    • Do you have any problems with your physical health? Use the section on Eliciting medical review of systems if neuropsychiatric, somatic symptom and related disorder, or manufactured illness is suspected.   
    • What about in the past? Any medical illnesses? 
    • Have you ever had any operations or been in the hospital? 
    • Have you ever had any accidents, head injury or loss of consciousness?


G. Current medications


● What medications do you take regularly? 

● What medications have you had in the past? 

● Are you allergic to any medications?


H. Substance use history


    • To better understand your condition I need to know whether you have any addictions. Can I ask you about that?
    • Do you smoke or chew tobacco? 
      • How much and how often in a day? 
      • Tell me more about it
    • Do you use alcohol? 
      • Use the section on Eliciting alcohol use history for further questioning
    • Have you ever used any recreational drugs such as cannabis, cocaine/crack, amphetamines, speed, ecstasy, LSD (or) acid?
      • Use the section on Eliciting illicit drug use history for further questioning 


I. Family history


‘Now, I would like to ask you a few questions about yourself and your background.’ 


    • Tell me about your parents. 
    • How old are they? 
    • What work did your parents do? 
    • How did you get along with your parents? 
    • How did your parents get along together? 
    • Do you have any brothers or sisters? Tell me about them. 
    • As far as you know, has anyone in your family or blood relatives ever had problems with their mental health? 
    • Has anybody had problem with alcohol, epilepsy or history of suicides? 


J. Personal, social and forensic history


‘I would like to talk now about your childhood, education and adolescence.’


    • Childhood 


      • Where were you born? 
      • Where were you brought up? And by whom? 
      • As far as you know was your mother’s pregnancy normal? 
      • Was it a normal delivery? 
      • Were there any problems around the time of your birth or when you were growing up? 
      • Were you walking and talking at the correct times? 
      • Did you have any serious illness as a young child?
      • When did you start school?
      • How were you in studies?
      • Did you have many friends at school?
      • Did you have any problems at school? truancy, expulsions, difficulty in getting along 
      • Have you ever been bullied or did you bully others? 
      • How far did you get in school?
      • When did you finish school? Reasons for leaving early?
      • How would you describe your childhood? Ask in particular about: 
        • Any major loss during childhood 
        • Separations 
        • Childhood neglect 
        • Physical/emotional/sexual abuses in childhood 
        • Sibling rivalry


    • Work


      • What work did you do? For how long? Then what happened?
      • How many steady jobs did you have?
      • What is your current job? How do you feel about your current job? Any problems with the job/colleagues? 
      • Are you unemployed at the moment? If so, why?


    • Psychosexual 


      • Are you married or in a relationship?
      • How do you get on with her? Any difficulties in your current relationship? 
      • Do you have children? If so, how many, and how old are they? 
      • How is your relationship with your children? 
      • Any previous relationships have you had? 
      • Tell me more about your previous relationships, previous partners/ separations/divorces?


    • Social 


      • Who lives at home with you at the moment? 
      • Do you have friends or family who live nearby?
      • Where do you live? Since how long? Own/rented house?
      • What is your current source of income?
      • Do you have any worries about debt or money in general?


    • Forensic


      • Have you ever been in trouble with the police or broken the law?
      • Have you ever been charged or convicted of anything? 
      • Have you ever been involved with any criminal activity? 
      • If any, what are the offences/crimes that you have committed? 
      • Have you served any period in prison/probation/on remand?
      • Try to obtain a list of offences, charges, legal outcome and length and place of any prison sentence.


K. Premorbid personality


Start with open questions: 


    • How would you describe yourself as a person before you were ill? 
    • How do you think other people would describe you as a person? 


Then ask closed questions about individual personality traits: 


    • Predominant mood: 
      • Optimistic/pessimistic 
      • Stable/prone to anxiety 
      • Cheerful/despondent 
    • Interpersonal relationships: 
      • Current friendships and relationships, previous relationships – ability to establish and maintain 
      • Family relationships 
    • Coping strategies: 
      • When you find yourself in difficult situations, what do you do to cope?
      • What sort of things do you like to do to relax? 
    • Personal interests: 
      • Hobbies and interests 
      • Use of leisure time 
    • Beliefs – religious beliefs 
    • Habits – food, fads, etc. 


    • Cluster A personality
      • Do you prefer being on your own? (schizoid) 
      • Do you find it difficult to trust people? (paranoid) 
      • Do you believe in the supernatural, or that you have special powers? (schizotypal) 
    • Cluster B personality
      • Do you find it difficult controlling your emotions? (core feature of cluster b personality disorders) 
      • Asking about borderline traits: 
        • Do you often feel empty on the inside?
        • Do you ever hurt yourself in response to stressful situations?
        • Do you often think about ending your life?
        • Do you tend to have intense relationships?
        • Are you often worried about being abandoned?
    • Cluster C personality 
      • Are you an anxious person?
      • Do you find it hard to be alone? (dependent) 
      • Do you often worry about being rejected, or think that you’re not as good as other people? (avoidant) 
      • Are you a perfectionist? (obsessive compulsive)
