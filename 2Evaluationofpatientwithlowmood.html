Evaluation of a patient with low mood 

[Expanded with example questions to ask and MSE findings to expect]


Scenario: A 55 year old man who is not sleeping well and feels tired. His wife passed away 7 months ago.


Checklist:


Introduction

  • Introduces self
  • Explains purpose of history and gains consent


Focused history of presenting complaint

  • Open question “What brings you here today? What sorts of problems have you been having”
  • Complaint: onset, sudden/gradual?, preceding event?


Mood history: Depression

  • Mood 
    • Asks about mood? 
      • 'Of late how has your mood been?'
    • Asks about how long they have been feeling this way?
      • 'How long has it been that way?' 
    • Asks if mood is worse OR vary at any time of the day 
      • 'Is the mood worse at any particular time of the day?'
    • Asks about easily moved to tears or easy irritability? 
      • 'Do you have crying spells?' 
      • 'Do you get easily irritated?'
  • Interest
    • Asks if the patient still enjoys the way they used to enjoy things [anhedonia]
      • 'Since onset of these symptoms have you been experiencing little interest or pleasure in activities that you used to do earlier?'
  • Sleep
    • Asks the patient about their sleep?
      • 'How is your sleep?'
    • Asks clarifying questions about sleep trouble? [initial / middle / late insomnia]
      • initial - 'Do you have trouble falling asleep?'
      • middle - 'How many times do you wake up in the night? Can you go back to sleep again?'
      • late - 'What time do you wake up in the morning? [Look for early morning awakening]
  • Appetite
    • Asks the patient about their appetite?
      • 'Has your appetite changed recently? If yes Do you tend to eat less or more?'
  • Weight
    • Asks the patient about change in weight?
      • 'Has your weight change recently? If yes Have you lost or gained weight? How many Kgs?'
  • Energy
    • Asks the patient about change in energy level / easy fatigue? 
      • 'How have your energy levels been? Do you feel tired most of the time?'
  • Libido
    • At this point it is not expected that UG students explore about sexual symptoms. But the following way would be a sensitive way to ask about them.
      • I would like to ask you some sensitive questions such as sexual problems as depression may affect sexual function. Is it OK with you?
      • ‘Have there been any changes in your sexual function recently? 
      • If yes, can you tell me more about that?
      • When did it start? 
  • Concentration
    • Asks the patient about change in ability to concentrate?
      • 'How has your concentration / memory been?'
      • ‘Have you been having any difficulty making decisions?’
  • Cognitive triad of depression
    • Asks the patient about how they feel about self / What kinds of thoughts they have? [self-view]
      • 'How do you see yourself?'
    • Asks about how they feel about people / mingling to them? [people]
      • 'How do you feel about people around you?'
    • Asks about how they feel about future? [future]
      • 'How do you feel about your future?'
    • Clarifies from patient about ideas of hopelessness, helplessness, worthlessness, guilt [If required]
      • 'So, from what I gather....'
      • Worthless – 'Do you see yourself as a failure?'
      • Hopeless – 'Do you see feel hopeless about the future?'
      • Helpless – 'Do you feel nothing can help you?'
      • Guilt – 'Do you feel guilty or that you deserve punishment?'
  • Risks
    • For Low mood, Suicidal ideation assessment can be done at this juncture, but to maintain format it has been shown below
  • Effect on functioning
    • Asks for the impact of illness on day to day functioning?
      • 'What has been the impact of illness on your day to day functioning?'


Focused Psych ROS 

  • Anxiety 
    • 'Do you worry or lot or have panic attacks?'
  • Psychosis – Hallucinations & Delusions? 
    • 'When people are under stress, they complain of hearing voices or believing that other people are doing something to harm him. Do you have such experiences?’
  • Manic symptoms
    • 'In the past week, have you felt very cheerful or unusually irritable or over-talkative?'
  • Any other if required


Risks - Suicidal ideations

  • Shows empathy before asking about suicidal ideations
    • Use empathy statement like 'You have been going through a lot.'
  • Asked patient about death wishes?
    • 'Have you ever felt like life has become a burden? 
    • 'Do you ever feel you go to sleep and never wake up?'
  • Asked patient about suicidal ideations?
    • 'Have you ever has thoughts of harming yourself?'
  • Asks about any attempts
    • Have you ever attempted to harm yourself?
  • Asks about protective factors
    • What things stop you from harming self?


Past psychiatric history – esp depression, mania, suicide attempt

  • 'Have you ever had any psychiatric illness / consultation in the past?' [Explore in detail if yes]


Family history – esp. Depression, suicide, psychosis 

  • 'Has any of your blood relative suffered from any psychiatric illness or attempted suicide?'


Past medical history – including allergies, if required developmental history

  • 'Have your doctor diagnosed you with any medical condition?'
  • 'Do you have any allergies?'


Drug and Substance use

  • Prescription drugs
    • 'Are you on any prescription medications?'
  • OTC and CAM drugs
    • 'Are you using any OTC or Alternative medications?'
  • Substance of abuse – alcohol, tobacco, opioids, cannabis etc
    • 'Do you use any substance like alcohol, tobacco or recreational drugs?'


Psychosocial History

  • Education
    • 'How far did you go in school / college?'
    • 'How did you do in school / college?'
    • 'Did you have any trouble with peers / teachers?'
  • Occupation
    • 'What did you do after finishing school / college?'
    • 'What jobs have you held and for how long?'
    • 'Can you tell me about your current job?'
  • Family and friends, social support [who is providing], financial support, housing situation
    • 'Can you tell me about your family and friends?'
    • 'Can you tell me about who is providing emotional support to you at this moment?'
    • 'Do you have trouble making ends meet?'


  • Legal history?
    • 'Have you had any convictions or legal troubles?'


Insight 

  • 'Do you think you need help?'
  • ‘Would you agree if I say your current condition may be explained by an illness?’
  • 'Could it be a psychiatric illness?'
  • 'Would you be willing to accept treatment?'


Summary 

  • Thank patient 
  • Questions? Concerns?
  • Suggest further tests


Examiners global rating [5 marks] based on

  • Good question style [open / closed / clarifying]
  • Establishment of rapport
  • Use of empathic statements
  • Clarity [non-jargon, pace and tone]
  • Courtesy
  • Non-judgmental attitude
  • Appropriate body language
  • Overall competence 



MSE findings in a patient of low mood


Appearance and Behaviour: Stooped or down cast posture, poor eye contact, psychomotor retardation, or sometimes restlessness. Catatonia may be rarely present. 


Speech: Decreased volume, slow rate, and normal rhythm; speech may not be spontaneous.


Mood: Often described as “depressed,” “sad,” or “irritable.”


Affect: Constricted in dysphoric range, but congruent with the patient’s reported mood.


Thought process: Linear and goal-directed in the majority of patients, but often impoverished.


Thought content: Ruminations of guilt about the past, hopeless about the future, poverty of content, or paranoid ideation; content disturbance may reach delusional proportions in depression with psychotic features.


Suicidality: Suicidal ideation is present in more than half of depressed patients. A plan needs to be specified if present. Assess how the patient manages or resists suicidal impulses.


Homicidality: May occur with psychotic features.


Perceptual: Auditory, command or visual hallucinations may occur with psychotic features.


Cognition: Oriented, some problems with immediate recall (registration), but not delayed recall; concentration is often poor, and language deficits are rare. The patient may have a good fund of knowledge and vocabulary, without disturbance in abstract thinking. The mini-mental state exam score should be normal unless depressive pseudodementia is present. 


Insight: Distorted, but not poor with exaggerated emphasis on depressive symptoms.


Judgment: Often impaired by the intensity of depressive symptoms.
